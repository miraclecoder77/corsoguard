# Cane Corso Health in 2026: The New Genetic Screening Standards Every Owner Needs to Know

If you've owned a Cane Corso for more than a few years, you already know the anxiety that comes with it. Not the "will he behave at the dog park" kind—the deeper kind. The 2 a.m. Google spiral about hip dysplasia, the quiet dread every time he gets up slowly from the floor. These are big dogs with big hearts and, unfortunately, a few well-documented health vulnerabilities that come with the territory.

The good news? The science has genuinely caught up. In 2026, genetic screening for large breeds isn't some niche option for show dog breeders anymore—it's becoming standard practice for responsible ownership. And if you're not familiar with what's now available, this article is going to change how you think about your Corso's long-term care.



## Why Corsos Are More Vulnerable Than People Realize

Let's start with the honest version of things. The Cane Corso is a relatively healthy breed compared to some other giant breeds—but that's a low bar, and "relatively healthy" shouldn't make anyone complacent.

The issues that show up most consistently in Corsos fall into a few categories: orthopedic problems (hip and elbow dysplasia, primarily), cardiac conditions, and a handful of hereditary conditions that have historically flown under the radar because they weren't always testable. Bloat—or gastric dilatation-volvulus—is another serious risk for deep-chested breeds like this one, and it can be fatal without immediate intervention.

What's changed recently is our ability to *predict* rather than just *react*. That shift is huge. A dog that shows no symptoms at age two might be quietly developing joint deterioration that a physical exam won't catch for another three years. Genetic markers, when identified early, let you get ahead of it.



## DSRA Testing: What It Is and Why It Matters for Your Corso

The DSRA—DNA Structural Risk Assessment—is one of the more significant developments in canine genetic health screening to emerge in recent years. Without getting too deep into the biology, it works by analyzing specific gene variants linked to connective tissue integrity, joint formation, and inflammatory response pathways.

For a breed like the Cane Corso, where hip and elbow dysplasia are consistent concerns, this kind of screening is genuinely valuable. A DSRA panel can flag elevated risk for joint degradation *before* you see any clinical signs—before the limping, before the reluctance to climb stairs, before you're looking at a dog in pain who needed intervention two years ago.

The test itself is straightforward—most labs now work from a simple cheek swab, and results typically come back within two to three weeks. What you're looking for in the results isn't a pass/fail so much as a risk profile. Some dogs come back low-risk across the board. Others show elevated markers in specific areas, which tells you and your vet exactly where to focus—whether that's tailored supplementation, modified exercise protocols, or more frequent imaging as the dog ages.

If your Corso is a puppy, this is the ideal time to run it. If they're older and you've never done genetic screening, it's still worth doing—knowing the landscape helps you make smarter decisions going forward, even if you can't turn back the clock.



## Hip Scoring in 2026: The Standard Has Shifted

Hip scoring has been around for decades, but the methodology and expectations around it have evolved considerably. The two main frameworks—OFA (Orthopedic Foundation for Animals) and BVA/KC hip scoring (more common in Europe)—are still the reference points, but what's changed is how breeders and vets are integrating scoring with genetic data.

In 2026, a hip score alone is increasingly seen as incomplete. It's a snapshot—a single X-ray read at a single point in time. Paired with a DSRA result, though, it becomes part of a much more useful picture. A dog with a borderline hip score but low genetic risk for progressive joint disease is a very different situation from a dog with the same borderline score and elevated markers for connective tissue fragility.

For Corso owners, the practical takeaway is this: if your breeder didn't provide hip scoring documentation with your dog, push for it at your next vet visit. Most vets can facilitate OFA evaluations directly. And if you're buying from a breeder in 2026 who *isn't* providing both hip scores and genetic health panel results? Walk away. That standard exists now, and reputable breeders know it.


## Joint Supplements: What's Actually Worth Your Money

This section could easily become a rabbit hole, so let's keep it practical. The supplement market for dogs is enormous and largely unregulated, which means there's a lot of noise to cut through.

For Corsos specifically—especially those with any genetic or physical indicators of joint vulnerability—the compounds with the most consistent evidence behind them are glucosamine and chondroitin (usually sold together), omega-3 fatty acids from fish oil, and more recently, UC-II collagen, which works through a different mechanism than traditional glucosamine and has shown solid results in clinical trials for canine osteoarthritis.

What doesn't have great evidence behind it? A lot of the trendy "joint blends" with fifteen ingredients and vague claims. More isn't always more. Pick something with clear dosing, recognizable active ingredients, and ideally a product formulated for large breeds—because dosing for a 110-pound Corso is very different from dosing for a 40-pound dog.

One thing worth noting: supplements are supportive, not corrective. If your Corso already has significant joint issues, supplementation alone isn't enough. It works best as part of a broader management plan that includes appropriate exercise, weight management (Corsos can pack on pounds fast, and every extra pound is extra joint load), and regular veterinary monitoring.


## The Case for Keeping Digital Health Records

Here's something that doesn't get talked about enough—the organizational side of all this. You can run every test, do every screening, get every score... and then have it all scattered across three different vet offices, a folder on your phone, and a manila envelope somewhere in a filing cabinet.

That's a problem. Especially for a breed where health history genuinely matters—for your own decision-making, for specialists if you ever need a referral, and for any future owners if your dog's circumstances ever change.

A centralized digital health record changes all of that. When your Corso's DSRA results, hip scores, vaccination history, supplement protocols, and vet notes all live in one organized, accessible place, you're not just being tidy—you're building a clinical picture that makes every future health decision easier and better informed. Some owners are now using dedicated platforms built specifically for this purpose, and the difference between managing your dog's health reactively versus proactively comes down, in large part, to having the information at your fingertips when you need it.

Think of it like a health vault. Everything in one place, always current, always accessible. For a dog you're going to love for the next ten or twelve years, it's worth setting up properly.



Start with the screening. Build the records. Stay ahead of it. Your Corso can't advocate for himself—but you can.
